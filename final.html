If a non-native aquatic plant is introduced into a new environment, there is a potential for ecological and environmental harm to the native landscape. When gone unchecked, this can entirely destroy a biome from the bottom up, and eradication of the plant can be nearly impossible.

By educating ourselves on how to spot and report an invasive plant species, we can prevent future outbreaks and keep our native populations and beautiful landscape thriving.

COMMON SPECIES

Curly Pondweed

> Wavy, lasagna-like leaves
> Leaves have mid-vein toothed edges
> Flowers grow about one inch above water
> Rhizome roots shoot directly from stem

Purple Loosestrife

> Lance shaped leaves
> 5-6 magenta petals, yellow centers
> Each produce 2.7 million seeds annually
> Thick, woody roots

Flowering Rush

> Tall leaves, twist near the tip
> Three pink petals & tree sepals
> Most reproduce by vegetative spread
> Bulb-like roots

HOW YOU CAN HELP

KEEP IT CLEAN

Remove all visible plants and drain any excess
water from water-related equipment before
leaving a body of water.

Allow your equipment to dry out fully before using
it again in a different location.

SMART DISPOSAL

Never dispose of bait or foreign materials into a
body of water. Always dispose of any bait into
a trash can.

If you want to keep your bait for later, replace
the water in your container with bottled
or distilled water.

REPORT SIGHTINGS

Each Minnesota region is assigned as AIS specialist
responsible for preventing future outbreaks. A full
list is available at dnr.state.mn.us.

If you suspect a plant may be invasive, note the
exact location, take a photo of the specimen,
and call the AIS specialist in your region.

REACH OUT

Reach out to your representatives to learn more
about progress being made in your area. If there
is no current organization, consider starting one!

Most invasive species are discovered by the
community. Talking with like-minded individuals
can heighten the chances of long term change.
